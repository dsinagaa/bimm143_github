Background

All functions in R have at least 3 things:

  • a name (we pick that and use it to call the function)
  • input arguments (one or more comma separated inputs that go inside the brackets when we call the function)
  • the body (the lines of Rn code that dot eh work of the function).

Q1. Write your first R function: add()

Here we will create a function to add some numbers. Let’s call it ‘add()’.

Input arguments can be either “required” or “optional”. The latter have fall-back default values that will be used if the user does not satisfy them.

Q5a. At which sequence length do your randomly generated peptides start to look “unique in nature” (i.e. no 100% coverage + 100% identity hit)?

They start to look “unique in nature” when the sequence length is 8 amino acids.

Q5b. Speculate why very short random peptides are almost always found in nr, while longer ones typically are not. Your answer should refer both to the size of the sequence space (20𝐿 for a peptide of length 𝐿) and to the size of the known protein universe

The possible amino acid combinations of shorter peptides (smaller L) is less than that of longer peptides (larger L). Thus, it is more likely for the shorter peptides to match a sequence already known in the protein universe. In other words, very short random peptides are almost always found in nr because they are more likely to match with a sequence in the database.

Q6. Connecting your findings to immunology (MHC class II and T-cell activation)

Q6. In 3–6 sentences total and using your Q5 data and the reasoning from Q5b, what do you think this minimum length is and why might it be a bad design choice for the immune system to present very short peptides?

Based on the data from Q5, the minimum length for peptide binding is eight amino acids. It might be a bad design choice for the immune system to present very short peptides because it is more likely for them to mistakenly match with a region of a sequence compared to large peptides. The T cells might mistake foreign (non-self) peptides as self peptides. If this happens, the immune response won’t be activated and the potentially dangerous cells won’t be killed. It is also possible that the T cells mistake self peptides as non-self. If this happens, the immune response kills their own healthy cells.
